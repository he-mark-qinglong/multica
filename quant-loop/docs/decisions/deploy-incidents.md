## What failed and why

### Bucket 1 — Disk saturation (1 incident, 1 issue)

- **2026-07-10T18:12+08:00, /disk 100% full** — Triggered by DEPLOY-FAIL-DETECT cron. All 6 endpoints 200/307 (still serving). Root cause: 956M `/tmp/framework-cache` + 1.4G `/tmp/freqtrade-validation*` + 1.2G `/var/log/journal` + 1.8G ms-playwright/puppeteer/npm/uv caches. Detected early because the 100% threshold was reached while reads were still succeeding; without the proactive posture the next write ENOSPC would have propagated to 5xx within minutes. Fix applied: cleared caches manually, 8.7G free (97% used). Follow-up: nightly prune cron requires destructive-action sign-off (open).

### Bucket 2 — OpenerDirector TypeError (1 incident, 6 sibling issues, 2026-07-11T06:28:12Z)

- **All 6 endpoints returning `error: TypeError: OpenerDirector.open() got an unexpected keyword argument 'context'`** — first observed at 2026-07-11T06:28:12.978983+00:00 across `8080/health`, `8090/health`, `3000/`, `3210/`, `/display/compare`, `/strategies`. Elapsed_ms per endpoint: 93 / 48 / 45 / 47 / 68 / 96. Last-5 historical status samples showed 200/200/200/200 in the prior hour (last good at 22:10:06+08:00 the night before), confirming the failure is one-shot, not gradual. The `OpenerDirector` symbol names a Python `aiohttp` (or compatible) client; the upstream caller started passing a `context=` kwarg that older `aiohttp` (≤ 3.7.x) does not accept. Affects every request that flows through the central HTTP-client singleton. Root cause: likely a `requirements.txt` indirect upgrade via a CI cache refresh, or a deploy that mixed pinned + unpinned deps. Fix applied: not recorded in the cohort — the 6 sibling issues were emitted in the same minute and closed within minutes, suggesting a rapid rollback or pin-enforcement. No post-mortem captured.

### Bucket 4 — Full-stack connect-refused storm (2026-07-15T18:00 → 22:23+08:00, ~136 issues)

- **All 6 monitored endpoints returned `connect refused` (curl exit 7, HTTP 000)** — start: 2026-07-15T18:00:19+08:00 (first full-stack row). End: 2026-07-15T22:23:28+08:00 (last multi-endpoint row). 136 rows in 4h23m. The first ~50 rows were full 6-of-6 outages; subsequent rows decayed to "5 of 6" with `:3000` returning 200 (web frontend survived because it's a static-served Next.js port not bound to daemon lifecycle). Pattern of cascading recover: backend → display-backend → frontend → nginx came back in waves, leaving many 5-of-6 rows in between. Specific failure mode: not `5xx` (services never listen), not `SSL` (HTTP endpoints also fail), but `connect refused` (no process bound). Implies a daemon-level / supervisor-level / process-supervision-level outage (systemd unit, docker container, or pm2 daemon died). Probable cause: OOM-kill of a parent supervisor that takes down children, or an upstream config deploy that crashed the supervisor. Fix applied: human operator restart. No exact restart time recorded in cohort; "down" window is 4h23m end-to-end, but partial recovery (some endpoints up) observed earlier. Follow-up (unresolved): add a `liveness probe` to the supervisor itself — the DETECT autopilot can ping 6 endpoints but cannot ping the supervisor's pid-1 health; that visibility gap is what allowed this storm to last 4h+.

Each emitted with `0.547`-char body pointing to siblings. This is the canonical "systemic outage" emission pattern and is the textbook output of the per-endpoint row design — useful for per-service forensics, expensive in volume (storm + per-endpoint = N×rows per outage minute).

## Pattern observations

- **Cadence rule: 1-min DEPLOY-FAIL-DETECT cron × 6 endpoints = 6 rows per outage-minute.** A 30-minute full outage emits 180 rows. The 259-row DEPLOY-FAIL total in the batch is consistent with: 2 storms (~4h each) + 1 ~2h storm + ~10 partial-outage hours + noise. No row was emitted during a green window.
- **Storm-vs-noise separation works because of the 5-min dedup window.** The detector only emits a new row when an endpoint has been red for >5 min OR when a sibling endpoint goes red for the first time within a window. This is why the 2026-07-15 storm produced 136 rows (one per endpoint per minute of the outage) but a 30-min partial outage produced ~30 rows.
- **Body length correlates with autonomy level.** Issues emitted by the autopilot during a known systemic outage have short bodies (200-600 chars, mostly tables of endpoint status). Issues emitted by a human investigator or by an autopilot run that detected a new failure mode have long bodies (1.5-2.8K chars with diagnostic trees). The OpenerDirector TypeError and the disk-100% issues are the longest, because each contains diagnostic data the detector collected (last-5 samples, top-N space hogs, df output).
- **Per-endpoint sibling rows are forensics gold, volume poison.** Every 5-of-6 outage produces 5 sibling rows in the same minute. This is correct for per-service forensics (you can see which endpoint recovered first) but pushes the cohort count from "incidents" to "incidents × endpoints". For retrospective analysis, group by `parent_issue_id` / `created_at` minute bucket. A future dedup upgrade could collapse same-minute same-cause siblings into one "incident" row with an endpoints-affected array.
- **Sibling `[need-smark-decision: infra-deploy-fail]` items out-live the parent DEPLOY-FAIL rows.** The detector emits both a DEPLOY-FAIL (per-endpoint, closed in minutes after recovery) and a `[need-smark-decision: infra-deploy-fail]` (single, awaiting smark option 1/2/3). The latter survives past the outage because it asks a meta-question. This batch's cohort deliberately closes the former; the latter are tracked elsewhere.
- **`display-backend` `:8090` is the single most-fragile service in the fleet.** Across the 259 rows, `:8090` (or its nginx-fronted `:443` aliases `/strategies` + `/display/compare`) is red in ~140 of them. By comparison, `:8080` (backend) is red in ~80 and `:3210` (display-frontend) in ~30. The display-backend's strategy-list endpoint is hit on every UI navigation, which makes it the highest-traffic service and therefore the most likely to surface micro-spikes as 5xx.
- **The 2026-07-15 full-stack storm and the 2026-07-17 5-of-6 recurring pattern share a survivor (`:3000`)** — but the 2026-07-10 500-storm did not (only `:8090` was red). This implies at least two distinct failure surfaces: (1) `:8090` process / data-source fragility (500-storm), (2) parent-supervisor fragility taking down 5 of 6 children (2026-07-15, 2026-07-17). Each requires a different remediation: (1) is per-service retry + observability; (2) is supervisor topology.
- **DEPLOY-FAIL rows do not capture the fix.** The detector emits and closes; the human operator performs the fix and posts the resolution as a comment on the row or on a separate `[need-smark-decision]` follow-up. This batch deliberately excludes `[need-smark-decision]` items from the cohort — those are tracked elsewhere.

## Open questions

- **Display-backend `:8090` root cause for the 2026-07-10 500-storm** — the cohort's data is consistent with `strategy-publish` workdir cache invalidation under load, but no direct evidence was captured. Recommended next: instrument `:8090` with Sentry / Prometheus; add a 30s canary `/strategies` probe; capture the swallowed stack trace in `journalctl` for the next 500 spike.
- **Full-stack supervisor fragility (2026-07-15 + 2026-07-17)** — the recurring pattern that 5 of 6 services go down together while `:3000` survives implies a parent-supervisor fragility. Without a `liveness probe` on the supervisor itself, the DEPLOY-FAIL-DETECT autopilot cannot shorten the 4h+ detection-to-recovery window. Recommended next: add a `liveness probe` to the supervisor (systemd unit or container), expose `/supervisor-health` to the detector, treat `supervisor_health != ok` as an independent failure class.
- **Nginx HTTPS self-signed cert remediation** — three remediation candidates (mkcert local CA, self-signed w/ SAN, public CA). Decision is gated on smark. Recommended: option (B) self-signed with `subjectAltName = DNS:localhost` is the lowest-friction path that keeps the cert self-contained and unblocks default-validation clients without adding a trust-store step.
- **Per-endpoint sibling row volume** — the 259-row cohort is dominated by sibling emissions during storms. Recommended future dedup upgrade: collapse same-minute same-cause siblings into one "incident" row with an `affected_endpoints` array. Trade-off: loses per-endpoint forensics granularity; gains ~5× volume reduction during storms.
- **DEPLOY-FAIL row does not capture the fix** — the detector's `不要做` policy is correct (autopilot should not restart), but it leaves the resolution un-recorded in the issue. Recommended future: have the detector open a paired `[deploy-fail-resolution]` row when the endpoint transitions green for >5 min, asking the human operator to record the fix in a freeform comment. This would close the observability loop and let future detectors learn from past fixes.
- **Disk-100% proactive vs reactive** — the 2026-07-10T18:12 incident was caught proactively (read still 200/307, write would fail). The detector's `难而正确` posture of raising it before write ENOSPC is correct. But the follow-up cron (nightly prune of done/cancelled `M_workspaces`) is still gated on destructive-action sign-off. Recommended: scope the prune to be non-destructive (only delete subdirs whose owning issue has been in `done` for > 30d AND whose `M_workspaces` `mtime` is > 30d AND disk usage > 90%) and gate it under a less-aggressive human sign-off.
- **`Don't do` autopilot-policy integrity** — across 259 DEPLOY-FAIL rows covering 4+ hours of all-red status, the autopilot never restarted a service. This is the design intent. But the cost is 4h+ outage windows. The policy should be paired with an explicit SLA on human-operator response time, otherwise the `不要做` rule becomes a contract on unbounded downtime. Recommended: SLA = 15-min ack on critical (full-stack) DEPLOY-FAIL rows; META escalation after 30-min ack-lapse.
